üìö Definition: GTO (Game Theory Optimal)

GTO is a strategy that cannot be exploited by a perfectly rational opponent. It represents a theoretical equilibrium in poker. We study GTO to understand the baseline, but we exploit actual human errors.

Module 1: The C-Betting Framework and Range Advantage üìê

The Continuation Bet (C-Bet) is the cornerstone of post-flop strategy. A C-Bet is profitable not because you have a strong hand, but because your pre-flop raising range has a range advantage over your opponent's calling range on specific board textures.

1.1 Understanding Range Advantage (RA)

Range Advantage is determined by which player's range contains more hands that hit the board well (e.g., sets, two-pair, strong top-pair).

  • High Cards Boards (A-K-x, K-Q-x): The Pre-Flop Raiser (PFR) typically has a massive advantage here because they opened with all the strong Broadway cards, while the Big Blind defender's range is capped (it usually doesn't contain $A-A$ or $K-K$ if those were 3-bet). On these boards, you C-bet frequently with a small size (25%-33% pot).
  • Low/Connected Boards (7-6-5, 8-5-2): The Big Blind Defender often has a slightly uncapped or very connected range here, containing many smaller pocket pairs and suited connectors that make two pair and straights. On these boards, the PFR should check more and C-bet less, using a medium size when betting.
üìö Definition: Static vs. Dynamic Boards

A Static Board (e.g., $A\heartsuit K\spades 2\club$) is one where the turn and river are unlikely to change the rank order of hands significantly. A Dynamic Board (e.g., $9\heartsuit 8\heartsuit 7\spades$) is one where subsequent cards can easily create straights, flushes, or greatly improve equity, leading to greater volatility.

1.2 The Three Pillars of C-Betting

Every hand you C-Bet must satisfy one of these three roles to be GTO-compliant:

  1. Value Hands (V): Hands that are better than your opponent's continuing range (e.g., Top Pair, better). These bet to build the pot and extract money.
  2. Bluffs (B): Hands that have little current equity but excellent potential to improve (strong draws). These bet for fold equity and to balance your value bets.
  3. Protection/Thin Value (P): Medium-strength hands or draws that need protection from opponent equity (e.g., weak top pair, overcards with a backdoor draw). These bet with a small size to deny equity and extract thin value.
üí° Advanced Tip: GTO Small Sizing

Use the small 25%-33% sizing whenever you feel your range advantage is substantial and you want to bet a very high frequency (80-100%) of your hands. This low-risk bet defends your pre-flop equity and makes your opponent's life extremely difficult by forcing them to defend nearly their entire calling range.

1.3 GTO Sizing and Frequency Correlation

The size you choose dictates the required frequency and the shape of your range (merged vs. polarized):

üöÄ Fun Fact: The C-Betting Boom

During the early 2010s poker boom, the default strategy was to C-bet 100% of flops as the PFR. This was massively exploitable, leading to the development of the nuanced, frequency-dependent GTO strategy we use today.

1.4 Delayed C-Betting (Turn Strategy)

A Delayed C-Bet involves checking the flop and then betting the turn. This is crucial when the flop favors the Big Blind's calling range, but the turn card is favorable to the PFR.

  • Rationale: By checking the flop, you realize your equity for free and allow the BB to realize theirs. When a favorable card (like an overcard) hits the turn, your betting range is now much stronger than the BB's checking range, and you can apply pressure with a high frequency (80%+) bet.
  • Ideal Hands: Hands that have improved (hit a pair) or hands that had great backdoor equity that is now a strong draw or a thin value hand.

Module 2: Minimum Defense Frequency, Combinatorics, and Exploitation üîç

Mastering the GTO approach means understanding how often you need to bluff, how often your opponent needs to call, and how to count the exact number of hand combinations to ensure balance.

2.1 The Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)

MDF is the minimum percentage of your hands you must continue with (call, raise, or reraise) to prevent an opponent from profitably bluffing with any two cards.

üìö Definition: Effective Stack Size (ESS)

ESS refers to the smaller of the two players' stack sizes involved in a hand. All post-flop decisions, especially bet sizing and barreling plans, must be calculated relative to the ESS to manage the risk-to-reward ratio and ensure all-in bets land correctly on the river.

MDF Formula:

$$\text{MDF} = \frac{\text{Pot Size}}{\text{Pot Size} + \text{Bet Size}}$$

Example: If your opponent bets 50% pot (Pot: 100, Bet: 50, New Pot: 150), $\text{MDF} = \frac{100}{100 + 50} = 66.7\%$. You must defend 66.7% of your hands.

This formula is critical for bluffing. If you bet and your opponent is defending less than the MDF, you are bluffing too little, and they are folding too much. You should increase your bluff frequency.

2.2 Combinatorics: Balancing Ranges

To build a perfectly balanced range, you must count combinations (combos). The Value-to-Bluff Ratio is derived from MDF.

Value-to-Bluff Ratio ($V:B$ Ratio):

$$\text{V} : \text{B} = \frac{\text{Pot Size} + \text{Bet Size}}{\text{Bet Size}} - 1$$

Example: If you bet 100% pot ($\text{V}:\text{B} = \frac{1+1}{1}-1 = 1:1$). For every 1 value combo, you need 1 bluff combo.

  • Practical Application: On the river, after determining your $V:B$ ratio, you count how many combos of hands you have that beat your opponent's calling range (Value) and select the corresponding number of hands with no showdown value but good blockers (Bluff).
  • Hand Selection for Bluffs: Always prioritize hands that hold blockers to your opponent's strong calling hands (e.g., bluffing with the Ace of a missed flush draw blocks their ability to have stronger flushes or Ace-high combinations).
üöÄ Fun Fact: Combinatorics in Solvers

The concept of combinatorics is so crucial that modern GTO poker solvers primarily function by analyzing the frequency distribution of combinations across different betting lines, rather than just hand rankings.

2.3 Exploitative Adjustments: Leaks and Counter-Strategies

While GTO is the baseline, the highest win rates come from exploiting opponents who deviate from it.

  • Opponent A: The Calling Station (Under-folds MDF)

    Counter-Strategy: Virtually eliminate bluffs and bet thinner for value. If they call with worse than a middle pair, bet all your marginal hands that beat that middle pair. They pay you off.

  • Opponent B: The Folder (Over-folds MDF)

    Counter-Strategy: C-bet and barrel far more often with pure bluffs, especially using small sizings. Bet hands with zero equity, as the fold equity alone makes the bet profitable. Ignore the $V:B$ ratio.

  • Opponent C: The Check-Raiser (Overly Aggressive)

    Counter-Strategy: On the flop, check back more medium-strength hands (Top Pair/Weak Kicker, Middle Pair) to smooth-call their turn/river aggression. Bet your strongest hands and your pure trash to isolate their bluffing range.

üí° Advanced Tip: Exploiting Low MDF

When you notice an opponent folding 80% to your 50% pot C-bets (when MDF is 66.7%), you are leaving money on the table. To maximize exploitation, bet 100% of your range on that board texture, ignoring balance entirely, until they adjust.

This final module explores lines that require sophisticated range understanding, generating maximum fold equity and capitalizing on opponent mistakes.

3.1 Check-Raising: GTO Range Construction

A proper Check-Raising range must be balanced to prevent the PFR from exploiting your check-back range. This is primarily an Out-of-Position (OOP) strategy.

  • Flop Check-Raise Composition:
    • Value: All the nuts (sets, two pair, and sometimes the strongest Top Pair).
    • Bluffs: Primarily high-equity draws (Open-Ended Straight Draws, Flush Draws, and combo draws). You raise draws to create a larger pot for when you hit, or win the pot immediately.
    • Avoid: Check-raising with weak or medium-strength hands that have no significant draw; these should be check-called.
  • Turn Check-Raise: This line is highly polarized and only used when the turn card is highly favorable to your pre-flop calling range (e.g., the third heart or a straight completion). Your bluffs here are typically missed draws that still have a blocker to a better hand.
üìö Definition: Blockers (Card Removal)

A Blocker is a card in your hand that reduces the probability of your opponent holding a specific, usually strong, hand. For example, holding the $A\heartsuit$ when bluffing the river of a heart flush board reduces the number of full flushes your opponent can have, making them more likely to fold their inferior hands.

3.2 The Defensive and Offensive Donk Bet

A Donk Bet (leading into the pre-flop raiser) has evolved from a beginner mistake to a GTO-approved tool on specific board textures where the OOP player's range has a slight advantage.

  • Ideal Donk Boards: Wet, low-to-medium connecting boards, especially two-tone boards (e.g., $7\heartsuit 6\heartsuit 4\spades$). The BB has a high frequency of suited connectors and low pocket pairs that connect with this board, while the PFR often misses.
  • Donk Sizing: Almost always a small size (25%-33% pot). The small size keeps the range wide and prevents the PFR from cheaply raising or floating.
  • Range Composition: Include value (sets, two pair), protection (weak top pair), and high-equity draws. Hands like $7-6$ or $5-5$ are perfect candidates.
üöÄ Fun Fact: The Origin of the "Donk"

The term "Donk Bet" comes from older strategy where leading into the PFR was considered a leak characteristic of "donkeys" or poor players. Solvers have proven it is a small, necessary part of a balanced OOP strategy on specific boards, legitimizing the move!

3.3 Advanced Multi-Street Play: Triple Barreling and Runouts

Triple Barreling involves betting all three post-flop streets (Flop, Turn, River). This requires deep range foresight.

  • Bluff Barrel Selection: The hands you choose to bluff with must maintain their blocker value and equity as the board runs out. A hand that starts as an open-ended straight draw with an Ace blocker is an ideal candidate.
  • Runout Analysis: Only fire the third barrel (river) when the card is a "scare card" or a "blank".
    • Scare Card: A card that completes a potential draw (like a flush) but which you are blocking. The opponent is now pressured to fold their marginal made hands.
    • Blank: A card that changes nothing (like a 2 on an A-K-Q-T board). You bet to fold out hands that beat you (like middle pair) but that your opponent might be tempted to call with.
  • River Bet Sizing: River bets are often polarized and large (75% to 150% pot). This sizing optimizes your $V:B$ ratio for the highest expected value.
üí° Advanced Tip: Triple Barrel Trigger

The most powerful trigger for a triple barrel bluff is when the turn or river card is a card that improves your *perceived* range but not necessarily your *actual* hand. For instance, if you C-bet a high-card board and a middle card like a 9 hits the turn, barreling continues to represent hands like $A9$ or $K9$, making your opponent‚Äôs $QJ$ or bottom pair look worse.
